CHAPTER REFERENCE:
- Chapter 1 (Introduction): To establish that information is fundamentally different from physical objects—it's a relationship and action, not a noun—and that scarcity-based thinking about digital content is fundamentally flawed.
- Chapter 2 (DRM is a bad business-move for Microsoft): To demonstrate through Microsoft's case study how DRM systems create business vulnerabilities and strategic disadvantages even for the companies implementing them.
- Chapter 3 (Applying cryptography principles to DRM): To explain why DRM is cryptographically impossible by showing how it violates basic principles of secure cryptography, particularly the need for open review and the problem of shared secrets.
- Chapter 4 (DRM systems are bad for society): To show how anticircumvention laws create absurd legal situations that harm ordinary consumers and legitimate uses while expanding corporate control beyond reasonable bounds.
- Chapter 5 (DRM systems are bad for business): To demonstrate how DRM creates artificial market restrictions that harm competition and innovation by giving content creators veto power over hardware design.
- Chapter 6 (DRM systems are bad for artists): To reveal how DRM actually harms the very artists it claims to protect by using the VCR precedent to show how new technologies ultimately benefit creators despite initial industry resistance.
- Chapter 7 (The Internet as a copying machine): To establish that the Internet's fundamental nature is copying bits cheaply and quickly, and that successful digital publishing must embrace this reality rather than fight it.
- Chapter 8 (Information economy realities): To show that the information economy is real and beneficial, using examples from barbershops to Chinese Internet censorship to demonstrate how information flows create value.
- Chapter 9 (Metadata problems - Table of Contents): To introduce the systematic problems with metadata systems that parallel the problems with DRM—both rely on unrealistic assumptions about human behavior and system design.
- Chapter 10 (Metadata introduction): To define metadata and establish the typical utopian scenario that metadata proponents envision, setting up the systematic critique to follow.
- Chapter 11 (The seven problems with metadata): To systematically destroy the metadata utopia by showing how human nature—lying, laziness, stupidity, and self-deception—makes reliable metadata impossible.
- Chapter 12 (Reliable metadata approaches): To show that metadata can still be useful when approached realistically, with appropriate skepticism and understanding of its limitations, much like his approach to copyright reform.
- Chapter 13 (The future of reading and technology): To explore how evolutionary algorithms and accelerating technological change will reshape reading and content creation, connecting back to the book's themes about embracing rather than fighting technological change.
